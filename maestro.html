Maestro

Developing confidence and skill on the podium.

Role: User Experience Researcher and User Interface Designer
Timeline: January – December 2017
Project Type: Multidisciplinary Design Program Project
Methods: Personas, Usability Testing, Paper Prototyping, Digital Prototyping

Overview

Maestro is an interactive software tool that allows beginning conducting students to refine their conducting technique and build their confidence outside of the classroom without a live ensemble.

I am working with a team of 8 students from computer science, performing arts technology, music performance, and HCI backgrounds. This project originated in 2016, and this year, alongside a partner from UMSI, I've been working to evaluate last year's system's user experience, implement updates to the GUI, and develop recommendations for next year's team.

Problem Scope

At the beginner level, conducting students face two challenges: gaining control of gestural technique, and developing confidence on the podium. Due to the difficulty of finding musicians and ensembles to conduct live outside of the classroom, many students currently practice by conducting along to prerecorded music or reviewing their technique either in the mirror or through video recordings. These methods are passive, in that students follow the music. Conductors, however, influence the shape, style, and direction of sounds in real time.

Conducting students are learning how to lead music- not follow it- and there is currently a lack of tools to facilitate this learning process.

Requirements

Our team is sponsored by Dr. Andrea Brown, Assistant Director of Bands at the University of Michigan. The UX subteam developed the following requirements to ensure students in her Beginning Conducting course can practice effectively outside the classroom:

  • Develop a clearer lesson structure that can be navigated with ease
  • Provide users with enough feedback to allow them to discover different types of gestures controlling the attack, sustain, and release of each note conducted
  • Improve the visibility of lesson and calibration sequences
